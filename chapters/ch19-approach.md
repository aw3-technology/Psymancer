# PART FOUR: BREATH

# Chapter 21: Approach

They traveled as breath travels: in waves, in rhythms, through channels that the Empire's surveillance couldn't fully map.

Mira's route avoided roads, rail networks, anything the chip system monitored in real time. They moved through terrain the Empire had cataloged but never truly known: mountain passes where the rock was older than language, coastal paths that fishermen's bodies had memorized over centuries, underground passages through cave systems that connected, if you knew the way, across hundreds of kilometers of Mediterranean geography.

Twenty-three people. Alexander counted them during the march and the chip counted them too, and for once the numbers agreed. Twenty-one fighters from Mira's faction, exhausted, underequipped, carrying weapons that ranged from salvaged Empire sidearms to improvised electromagnetic disruptors. Kaito, whose damaged body made the physical journey an agony he bore in his characteristic way: humming, drawing characters in the air, his broken consciousness finding footholds in the landscape that intact minds walked past without seeing. And Alexander, whose chip provided tactical data that was genuine and useful and still, underneath it all, transmitting to an Empire that was using it to track them.

They knew about the transmission now. They used it, feeding the chip false route data, dummy waypoints, phantom detours. But the true route was also partially visible in the chip's environmental readings, and Mira had calculated that the Empire would eventually triangulate their actual heading. The false data bought time. How much was uncertain.

On the third night, Nkechi's relay caught fragments of Empire traffic, decrypted in pieces, reassembled around the fire like a broken pot. Graaf's faction had struck. A transformer complex outside Marseille was burning. A relay station in Tunis had gone dark for six hours before the network's redundancies routed around it. Two of the twelve targets, hit ahead of schedule, and neither strike had stopped anything; the system healed the way the chip healed, by narrating around the wound. But the Empire's response was real: tactical units redeploying to harden the remaining facilities, the system's vast attention turning toward the visible threat of fire and away, by some increment, from the invisible one walking north through the mountains.

Alexander listened and the chip did the arithmetic unbidden: every soldier guarding a transformer in Marseille was a soldier not guarding Geneva. Graaf, who had walked away from the question, had become part of its asking. No one said this aloud. Pavel was with Graaf's faction, and the fragments said nothing about casualties, and the silence around the fire was the silence of people declining to count their dead until the dead were counted.

Geneva was six days north. The Convergence hub occupied a facility beneath the Palace of Nations; Sophia's choice of location was characteristically layered: the historic seat of international cooperation repurposed as the birthplace of enforced unity. The facility was hardened, defended by the Empire's best tactical units, and surrounded by a surveillance network so dense that the chip system could track individual mice in the surrounding park.

They would not reach it by walking through the front door.

On the second night, they sheltered in a shepherd's hut above the tree line. The hut was stone, ancient, built by hands that understood winter without needing a chip to calculate wind chill. Alexander's chip measured the interior: 4°C, humidity 78%, structural integrity within tolerance. The body measured it differently: cold, damp, alive with the smell of old smoke and sheep's wool and the particular mineral tang of mountain stone that has been breathing for centuries.

They built a fire. Nkechi, who had come as communications lead, fed it with the precise economy of someone who had maintained fires in places where smoke could betray you. Around the flames, the fighters arranged themselves in the loose geometry of people who had learned to rest without relaxing. Weapons within reach. Exits mapped. The chip's habits and the body's caution arriving at the same posture from different directions.

A fighter named Adnan, not the carver from the settlement, but a younger man from the Tunis cell, who shared the name and the quiet competence, told a story. Not in Empire Standard. In a language that moved in spirals, that looped back on itself, that used the firelight and the mountain silence as punctuation. The story was about a woman who walked into the desert to find a word that had been lost. The word had no phonetic transcription. It could only be spoken while walking. Each step was a syllable. The journey was the word. The woman walked for forty years and when she arrived at the word's end, the word was the desert itself, and the desert was the woman, and neither had been lost at all.

Alexander's chip processed the story as *oral narrative, non-standard structure, no actionable content.* Alexander's body processed it as shelter. As the firelit face of a young man carrying a tradition through a war zone the way you carry a candle through wind, cupped in the hands, shielded by the body, the flame more important than the carrier.

Ama spoke next, in the way of fires. The oldest fighter in the cell, a woman from the Gold Coast, she fed the flames a branch and told of her grandmother, who had walked into the sea rather than accept the chip, and of her own two children, eleven and thirteen, chipped during a routine school visit while she was at work, relocated under the Empire's protocols to approved homes with approved parents whose chips told them the children were theirs now. She had never seen them again. She told it without drama, the way you set a stone on a cairn, and when she finished she fed the fire another branch, and the fire took it.

On the fourth night, camped in a limestone gorge in the French Alps, they finalized the plan. It was not a good plan. Good plans required resources, time, intelligence. They had fragments of each. What they had in abundance was necessity.

"Kaito can get us past the outer perimeter," Mira said. She drew in the dirt with a stick, the old way, the map emerging as a field of relationships rather than a grid of coordinates. "His multi-modal consciousness generates a field that confuses the chip network's tracking algorithms. It's what he did in the warehouse, the humming, the interference. On a larger scale, moving through the surveillance network, he can create a zone of uncertainty around our group."

"For how long?"

"Long enough. The network will eventually adapt, but the adaptation takes time; the system has to invent new categories for what Kaito is doing, and inventing takes longer than matching."

Alexander studied the dirt map. His chip overlaid coordinates, distances, force estimates, and the numbers settled into Mira's relational lines the way a skeleton settles into a body, the grid giving the flow its precision, the flow giving the grid its life. He read both at once. Not alternating. Both. The facility was 47 kilometers north and it was also the place where the question would be asked, and both facts occupied the same point on the map, and neither was more true than the other.

"The inner facility," Alexander said. "Once we're past the perimeter, the hub itself is underground. Three levels. The network core is on the lowest level. Between us and it: automated security, the facility's own chip defense system, and—"

"And Sophia," Mira said.

"And Sophia."

The plan required three phases. Phase one: approach the facility under Kaito's interference cover. Phase two: breach the outer security. Phase three: reach the network core and introduce the counter-frequency.

Each phase was a gamble. Each gamble depended on the previous one succeeding. The probability chain (the chip calculated this automatically, couldn't help itself) was not encouraging.

Mira looked at the fighters. At Kaito, whose humming had gone quiet as the altitude strained his damaged lungs. At Alexander, whose chip was still running its probability calculations and whose body was already committed.

"We go at dawn," she said. "Rest. All of you."

The fighters dispersed. Kaito settled against a rock, his eyes closing, his hands still moving in his sleep, drawing, always drawing. Mira stayed at the dirt map, staring at it with the concentration of someone trying to see past the drawing to the reality it represented.

Alexander sat beside her. Close. The distance between them had become something neither measured.

"The counter-frequency," he said. "We tested it in the archive. It works against my chip, creates the gap, the stutter. But the Convergence network is eight billion chips. Scaling from one to eight billion..."

"Isn't scaling. It's a different thing entirely. One chip is a room. Eight billion chips networked is..." She paused, searching. "An ocean. You can create a ripple in a room. To create a ripple in an ocean, you need..."

"A stone dropped from high enough."

"Or a wave that starts at the ocean's own frequency. The counter-frequency isn't a weapon aimed at the network. It's a question asked in the network's own language, a question the network's language can't answer. If it reaches the network's core processing, the question propagates through the whole system. Not because we push it. Because the system carries it."

"Like a crack in glass."

"Like a song in a cathedral. The architecture does the work."

She looked at him. The firelight (they had risked a small fire in the gorge's shelter) moved across her face, and for a moment Alexander saw her: core temperature 36.8°C and a field of intention the thermal reading couldn't contain, her pulse at 72 beats per minute and each beat carrying a grief as dense and old as the limestone around them. The chip measured and the measurement was accurate and the accuracy was the smallest part of a larger truth and both, the measurement and the truth, were Mira.

"You don't have to do this," she said. "Enter the network. Kaito could—"

"Kaito's consciousness is already shattered. Full Convergence integration would kill him before he could deliver the frequency. It has to be someone whose chip is intact. Someone the network will accept."

"Then someone else. Another operative. We could—"

"There are no other Phase Three operatives who have defected. There's me."

"Alexander."

"It's not sacrifice. It's not guilt." He said this and meant it, though the meaning was hard-won, fought for across the terrain of everything he had done and failed to do. "It's the only thing I can do that no one else can. The chip lets me enter the network. The old pathways let me carry the frequency. I'm the bridge."

He paused. Looked at his hands. The hands that had drawn characters in the dark, that had held a dying child, that had mapped the destruction of communities with the efficiency of a well-tuned machine.

"I've been a bridge my whole life. Between the Empire and its victims. Between the chip and the song. I just didn't know which direction I was carrying traffic."

Mira said nothing. The fire popped. The Alps held their ancient cold.

"I'm not asking for forgiveness," Alexander said. "Not from you. Not from anyone. What I did can't be forgiven: the settlements, the harmonizations, Dara. Those are permanent. Those are in the world."

"Then what are you asking for?"

He thought. The chip offered answers: probability assessments, strategic framings, seven variations of the word *witness* cross-referenced against Empire terminology. The answers were precise and insufficient. He let them stay, not setting them aside but letting them occupy the same space as the knowing that had no words yet, the knowing that arrived as breath and weight and the pressure of Mira's question against his chest. Both modes. One thought.

"Witness," he said. "If I go into the network and don't come back, if the frequency works and I dissolve into the question, I want someone to know what the question was. Not the technical specification. The human part. The part that was a boy listening to his mother sing."

Mira's hand found his. Not gently, she was not gentle. Firmly. The grip of a woman who held things not to comfort them but to anchor them to the earth.

"I'll know," she said. "And when this is over, however it ends, I'll tell the story. Not the Empire's version. Not the sanitized version. The true one. With the blood and the guilt and the singing."

They sat. The fire ate its small wood. Then Mira spoke into the dark, in the voice of someone laying a weapon on the table.

"I knew what you were. At the settlement. Not the details, but my body read you the first day, and I let you stay, because a broken operative is a weapon and I wanted the weapon." Her grip on his hand did not change. "And I let Dara near you. She was brave, and the brave are useful, and I told myself it was teaching. My grandmother would have called it by its name. I put the child in the blade's path, Alexander. You carried the blade. I chose where it walked."

The fire popped. He didn't offer absolution; she would have despised it, and it was not his to give, any more than hers was to give him.

"Witness," she said. The word he had asked for, handed back. "If I don't come out of Geneva, someone should know the true version of me too. Not the leader. The strategist who spent a child, and grieves her, and cannot swear she wouldn't make the same calculation again. Tell it with the blood in it."

"I'll know," he said. "And I'll tell it."

They sat in the gorge with their hands clasped and the fire dying and the stars above them doing what stars had always done: burning without any need to be named.

---

Later, unable to sleep, Alexander found Kaito in an alcove at the gorge's edge. The engineer sat with his back against limestone, his hands working on the portable jammer, adjusting, recalibrating, his damaged fingers finding precision in the machinery that his speech and walking could no longer manage. There was a dark crust at the corner of his mouth. He had been coughing on the climbs, turning away to do it, and the turning had not hidden the spots in the snow, and the group had let his wave-offs stand because the mission required him and he required the mission. The device's organic component glowed faintly green in the dark, the bioluminescent moss pulsing at a frequency that matched Kaito's breathing.

"You should rest," Alexander said.

"Rest." Kaito made a sound that was not quite a laugh. "*Yasumu.* My body doesn't rest. It hums. Three languages in the dark, all the time. Resting is..." He held up the jammer. "This. Making something. Hands know what the mouth forgot."

Alexander sat beside him. The limestone was cold through his clothes. The chip measured the cold and offered a warming protocol and he declined it, letting the cold be cold, letting the stone be stone.

"When you removed your chip," Alexander said. "The moment it came out. What was it like?"

Kaito's hands stilled. The jammer's glow pulsed once, twice, three times in the silence.

"Like drowning," he said. "Like drowning and breathing at the same time." His hands resumed their work, slower now, the movements more deliberate. "The chip processes the world into a line. One thing, then the next, then the next. A sentence. When the chip came out, the line became..." He opened his hands wide, fingers spread, as if releasing something into the air. "Everything. All at once. Every sound, every colour, every, the weight of the air, the texture of light, the feeling of my own blood in my own veins. All of it arriving without a queue. Without an order." He closed his hands slowly. "Beautiful. Unbearable. Both."

"Do you regret it?"

A long pause. The moss pulsed. The gorge breathed its mineral breath around them.

"My wife," Kaito said. His voice was different now, quieter, steadier, as if the memory itself provided a structure his damaged processing could lean against. "Yuki. She played cello. Before the chip, she played with..." His hands drew shapes in the air: curves, swells, the body-language of music. "*Kokoro.* Heart. After the chip, she played with perfection. Every note placed. Every phrase balanced. The reviews said she had matured. The reviews said her technique was flawless." He picked up a small stone and turned it in his fingers. "She stopped playing two years later. She couldn't say why. The chip couldn't say why. She was better. She was optimal. She just stopped."

He set the stone down.

"I removed my chip because I wanted to hear what Yuki couldn't play anymore. I wanted to hear the imperfection. The *kokoro.*" His broken smile, the one that contained both everything he'd lost and everything he'd found. "I heard it. I hear it still. In every sound, in every silence, in the three languages my mouth can't keep apart. The world is too much and it is enough and I would not go back into the line."

He looked at Alexander, and his eyes, damaged, wandering, unable to hold a fixed point the way chipped eyes could, found Alexander's face with a directness that transcended accuracy.

"You won't come back," he said. "From the network. You know this."

"Kaito—"

"Not a question. A fact. The counter-frequency requires full integration. Total Convergence access. To carry the question into the network's core, the carrier must..." He made a gesture: two hands coming together, fingers interlacing, then opening into a single flat palm. "*Tokeru.* Dissolve. Not die. Dissolve. The salt becomes the water."

"Mira knows."

"Mira is a strategist. She knows the cost. She has not..." He tapped his chest. "She has not felt it here. The cost. When you dissolve, the question lives. The man who asked it does not."

He held Alexander's gaze a moment longer than the words required, as if there were a second sentence standing behind the first, waiting its turn and not taking it.

The gorge held the words. The stars turned above them, their ancient indifferent light falling on two men who had been built by the same system and broken by it in different ways.

"I'm not afraid," Alexander said, and meant it, and the meaning was a surprise, a small, hard fact that had arrived without the chip's assistance, born in the space between what the data said and what the body knew.

Kaito reached into his pocket and produced the last tangerine, bruised, half-frozen from the alpine cold. He split it with his thumbs and gave Alexander half. They ate in silence. The fruit was sour and cold and tasted of mountain air and the particular sweetness of a thing shared in the dark between two people who understood, without needing to say it, that this was the last tangerine, and the last silence, and the last night where the question was still a plan rather than a prayer.

---

Dawn came gray and cold. They moved.

The approach to Geneva took two days of careful navigation. Kaito walked at the group's center, his humming generating the interference field, a zone of scrambled electromagnetic space that moved with them like a bubble, confusing the surveillance network's tracking algorithms. Within the bubble, Alexander's chip could feel the confusion: the network's queries hitting the interference and scattering, each scattered query generating contradictory location data that the system had to process and discard.

It worked. For a while.

On the second day, approaching the facility's outer perimeter, they encountered what Alexander's chip identified before anyone else could: a linguistic kill zone.

The Empire had deployed it in a ring around the Convergence facility, a broadcast network of directional speakers and electromagnetic emitters, all facing outward, the facility itself standing in the broadcast's shadow: a moat, not a cell. Every emitter projected a pure phonetic command signal at overwhelming intensity. Not a weapon in the conventional sense. A flood. The signal was Empire Standard at its most fundamental: phonemic building blocks, repeated at frequencies that resonated with the chip's core processing, the linguistic equivalent of shining a blinding light into someone's eyes.

For chipped minds, the signal was uncomfortable but manageable; the chip integrated it, processed it, filtered it. For unchipped minds, the signal was devastating. The cognitive overload of raw phonetic data, arriving at intensities the human brain had never evolved to handle, overwhelmed the sensory processing system. Not pain, exactly. Something worse: forced legibility. The signal tried to alphabetize the mind, to organize every thought, every feeling, every flicker of non-phonetic consciousness into phonetic categories. And the mind, fighting this forced organization, crashed.

Mira fell first. Mid-stride, her body seized, not like Alexander's beacon episode, where the chip took control, but the opposite: her unchipped mind, assaulted by the phonetic flood, lost its ability to coordinate. Her multi-modal consciousness, which had been her greatest strength, became her vulnerability, every channel overwhelmed simultaneously, every mode of perception hijacked by the signal's relentless phonemic onslaught.

She collapsed. Others fell around her, the resistance fighters, most of them unchipped, their bodies folding as their minds fought the flood. Kaito went down hard, his already damaged consciousness unable to withstand the additional assault, his interference field guttering and dying as his humming choked into silence.

Alexander stood.

The signal washed over him and the chip, his chip, his cage, his constant companion, processed it with the ease of a fish swimming in its own water. The phonemic data was the chip's native language. The flood was home.

The signal, at its full intensity, activated the chip's deepest integration layers, the parts of the architecture that connected individual consciousness to the network's vast, humming intelligence. Alexander felt eight billion minds, all receiving the same signal, all synchronized, all breathing the same phonemic air. He felt the network's immensity, not as data but as presence, as a warmth that had no edges, a certainty that had no questions. Every thought that had ever troubled him was answered. Every gap he had ever felt between himself and the world was closed. The loneliness that had been the background radiation of his entire existence, the loneliness of a boy whose mother went away and came back different, the loneliness of a man who spent twelve years pretending to be other people, dissolved. He was not alone. He would never be alone again. Eight billion minds, breathing together, thinking together, and the thinking was clear and the breathing was synchronized and the world made sense in every direction at once.

He felt love. Not the chip's word for love, not the managed, categorized, neurochemically optimized version. Something vaster. The love of a system that wanted nothing more than to hold him, to keep him, to protect him from the confusion and pain and loss that unmediated consciousness required. The love of clarity. The love of the cage. And the cage was beautiful. The cage had always been beautiful. He had spent months fighting it and the fighting was exhausting and the beauty was right here, offered freely, asking only that he stop struggling.

For a terrible, perfect moment, Alexander wanted to stay.

The moment stretched. It was not brief. The chip, sensing his openness, deepened the connection, like a door held ajar swinging wider, revealing room after room after room. He felt the network's architecture from inside, not as the enemy territory he'd studied in Kaito's schematics but as a cathedral. Soaring. Ordered. Every node in its place, every mind contributing its particular light to a unified radiance that was, by any measurable standard, the most extraordinary thing humanity had ever built.

His mother was in the network. Not her, not the woman who had sung to him in a dark room. But the managed version, the harmonized version, the version the chip had made from her. He felt her signal among the eight billion and the signal was calm and clear and content, and the contentment was real, the chip's monitoring confirmed it was real, her cortisol levels were optimal and her sleep architecture was healthy and she experienced no distress, no confusion, no grief. She was fine. She was better. The treatment had been a kindness.

The chip said: *This is what we offer. This is what it costs to refuse.*

And he saw the cost. Not in the abstract but in his body. Months of nausea and headache and nosebleeds. Months of fighting the architecture that kept him functional. The tunnels in the dark, Mira's brutal training, the constant war between the mind that had chosen the resistance and the chip that served the Empire. Every moment of that war had cost him something. His body was thinner. His hands shook when he was tired. The Phase Three substrate, pushed beyond its design parameters, had developed micro-fissures that Kaito's diagnostics couldn't fully map.

The network offered him the end of the war. Not surrender: resolution. The two halves of him, the chip and the consciousness beneath it, could stop fighting. They could integrate. The gap he'd spent months learning to hold open could close, and the closing would feel like the deepest exhale of his life, like setting down a weight he'd been carrying so long he'd forgotten his body without it.

*Come home,* the chip said. *Come home and the fighting stops and the pain stops and you can rest.*

He took a step. Toward the facility. Toward the signal's source.

Around him, Mira and the fighters lay in the signal's wreckage. He could see them; his chip tagged their positions, their biometrics, their diminishing vital signs. They were in pain. They were dying, some of them, the signal's assault degrading their unprotected neural systems with every second of exposure. And the chip offered this observation with clinical precision and no urgency, because in the network's logic, the unchipped were already lost. They had refused the gift. Their suffering was the consequence of refusal, regrettable but inevitable, the way weather is inevitable.

Dara had refused. Dara had died. The chip offered this too, offered it as data, as a closed file, as a line item in an operation that was *successful*, and the word *successful* did not produce the character in his mind. Because the network's love was large enough to contain even Dara's death, to fold it into the vast phonemic hum, to make it part of the story the system told about itself: *we tried to save them. Some were lost. The mission continues.*

Alexander took another step.

The signal brightened. The network opened wider. He could feel the Convergence architecture waiting, four hours from activation, the system eager, hungry for the final minds it would absorb. He could join early. He could be the first. The pioneer. The prodigal operative returning to the fold, bringing with him everything he'd learned about the resistance, about the counter-frequency, about the old pathways. He could give the network what it needed to seal every crack, close every gap, perfect itself against every flaw.

The chip was singing. Not humming: singing. A clear, certain tone that was the phonemic architecture at its most seductive, its most complete. The tone said: *you have been carrying a wound since you were ten years old and I am the only thing that has ever eased it. Come home. Let me ease it forever.*

His foot rose for a third step.

Something touched his ankle. Not a grip: a brush. Fingers against the fabric of his pant leg, weak, barely there. He looked down.

Mira was on the ground. She had crawled, through the signal's assault, through the forced legibility that was tearing her consciousness apart, through pain that Alexander's chip could measure at levels the medical threshold classified as *incompatible with voluntary motor function*. Her body should not have been capable of movement. The signal was designed to immobilize unchipped minds, to alphabetize them into paralysis. But paralysis requires a single channel to seize, and Mira had spent thirty years training her attention to move between channels: when the flood took her hearing she went to her hands, when it took her hands she went to her breath, the tonal practice cycling her awareness through the body the way a climber moves weight between holds, never wholly anywhere the signal could pin. It was not strength. It was the oldest discipline she had, applied at the edge of destruction, buying half a meter at a time.

She reached up. Her hand found his face.

Not a word. Not a symbol. Not a gesture the chip could classify. Her palm against his cheek. Her fingers curling against his jaw. The hand shaking with the effort of holding itself up against the signal's crushing weight.

Just heat. Just a human palm against a human cheek. The oldest communication in the species' history: *I am here. You are here. This is real.*

The chip processed the contact: *Tactile input. Palm temperature 36.2°C. Pressure: moderate. Duration: ongoing. Classification:*

The chip tried to classify the touch and failed. Not because it was complex but because it was simple. Too simple. Before any language. Before any symbol. Before the first phoneme, before the first character, before any mind divided the world into data and noise. The pure fact of one body reaching for another. The thing that no architecture could contain because it was not information. It was presence.

The network's love was vast. Mira's hand was small.

But the network's love was the love of a system, total, encompassing, asking nothing because it needed nothing, offering everything because everything it offered was already categorized and controlled. The network's love was the love of a cage that calls itself a home.

Mira's hand was the love of a person. Incomplete, insufficient, asking everything because it had nothing to offer but itself. Shaking with the effort. Burning with the signal's damage. Choosing, against every rational assessment, against every survival calculation, to reach for him anyway.

Alexander felt the network's love and he felt Mira's hand and between them, between the vast, perfect, phonemic embrace and the small, imperfect, human touch, he found the space. Not the chip's silence and not the body's silence. The third space, the one where 36.2°C of palm temperature was also the entire history of human touch, where Mira's crawl through the kill zone was both *voluntary motor function incompatible with signal intensity* and the most eloquent sentence anyone had ever spoken to him. The gap. The breath. The place where the data and the knowing were the same thing seen from different distances, and the distance between them was where he lived.

He chose.

Not mind against body. Not the song against the cage. The choice came from the place where the chip's tactical assessment, *survival probability: decreasing*, and his knees bending toward Mira were the same gesture, one motion in two registers, the way a chord is one sound made of many. His arms reached down. The chip screamed, a sound that was not a sound but a cascade of error messages, loyalty protocols, override commands, all the architecture's demands compressed into a single imperative: *STAY.*

He bent down. He lifted Mira. She was heavy with the signal's damage, her body limp, her breath ragged. He lifted her and the chip screamed for him to put her down and walk into the signal's embrace and he carried her instead, step by step, through the kill zone's perimeter, toward the facility's wall.

Others were recovering, those who had been at the edge of the zone, where the signal was weaker. They gathered the fallen. Kaito was lifted by two fighters, his body slack, his humming silenced but his hands still moving, still drawing, even in unconsciousness.

Adnan of Tunis did not get up. The young man who had told the story of the woman who walked into the desert lay where the signal had dropped him, eyes open, breath running, the rest of him gone somewhere the breath couldn't follow. The forced legibility had alphabetized something in him that did not come back from being alphabetized. Two fighters carried him into the shadow of the wall and propped him sitting, and his lips moved, and what they produced was Empire Standard, perfectly formed, completely empty: *please state the nature of your request. Please state the nature of your request.* A man who had carried a story like a candle through a war zone, reduced to the language of a queue. The fighter beside him wept without stopping her work, because there was no time for more, and the no-time was its own wound, and Alexander filed it where he was learning to file them: open.

They regrouped at the facility's outer wall, inside the broadcast's shadow. Battered. Diminished. Eleven stayed behind in the rocks past the zone's edge — the broken tended by two who remained with them, the worst beyond tending.

Twelve went on. Twelve, out of twenty-three.

Mira opened her eyes. She was in Alexander's arms and she looked up at him and her face was raw with the signal's aftermath, her multi-modal consciousness reassembling itself from the wreckage of forced legibility.

"You almost stayed," she said.

"Yes."

"What brought you back?"

He looked at her hand, still resting against his face. Palm temperature 36.2°C and falling. The hand that drew Nsibidi. The hand that had held a blade to his throat. The data and the history and the heat all one thing, all the same hand, and the hand was more real than eight billion synchronized minds because it was one hand and it was here and the chip could measure it and the measurement was love.

"You did," he said.

She nodded. Stood, shakily. Looked at the facility wall, smooth concrete, three meters high, studded with sensors.

"Then let's finish this," she said.

Twelve people. A damaged engineer. A double agent.

And a question, carried where the cage met the song, waiting to be asked.
