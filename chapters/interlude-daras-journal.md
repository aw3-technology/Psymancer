# Interlude: Dara's Composition Journal

*Found in the debris of the singing house, water-damaged, spine cracked, pages bent. Recovered by Empire archival specialist during post-operation material processing. Catalog number: MED-7-C-0041. Classification: juvenile symbolic practice, non-standard. Status: archived.*

---

**First entry. No date. The ink is dark, fresh, the characters pressed hard into the handmade paper.**

Sana says a composition journal is supposed to be private. She says you write only for the ceremony, only the characters that will go on the sand, and you don't let anyone read it, not even Mira, not until the day.

But I don't think that's right. Or I think it's right for Sana, who knew her composition the first time she picked up her brush, who drew it like she was tracing something already there. Sana's composition came to her finished. Mine comes to me in pieces, in arguments, in things I overhear and things I dream and the way the light moves on the wall above my bed when the wind pushes the shutters.

So this is not just for the ceremony. This is for me. For the parts of the composition that don't go on the sand, the parts that are underneath, the roots that hold the visible thing up. Mira says every character has a body you can see and a body you can't. The seen body is ink. The unseen body is everything the writer was when the brush moved.

I am writing my unseen body.

---

**Second entry. The handwriting is smaller, more hurried. A smudge of green pigment in the margin, herb residue, or garden soil.**

Today the machine man came.

He walked into the settlement the way people from the dead cities walk, with his eyes pointing at things instead of seeing them. Mira says the machines in their heads turn everything into words, and the words are like little boxes, and they put the world in the boxes and then they think they have the world. But they only have the boxes.

He is tall. His name is Marek but I don't think that's his name. I told Mira and she said she already knows and I should mind my composition and not mind the stranger. But Mira also said I should be his guide. She said: show him what we are. So either she wants him to see us or she wants us to see him seeing us. Mira always has two reasons. Sometimes three.

His hands are strange. They are the most controlled hands I have ever seen: every finger knows where it is, every movement has already been approved before it happens. Grandmother would say his hands have been taught to forget. But sometimes, when the singing is happening and he thinks no one is looking, his right hand moves on its own. His fingers curl and press and trace and the movement is not the machine's movement. It is older. It is like watching someone talk in their sleep in a language they don't know they speak.

I told him his hand wanted to draw during the practice. He looked at his hand like it belonged to someone else.

I think maybe it does.

---

**Third entry. The ink is a different color, darker, mixed with something that gives it a slight sheen. The characters are drawn with more care, as if practiced.**

Grandmother came to me last night. Not came — she is in the front room, same as always, mending the net Tomas tore on the rocks. But she came to me the way she comes when she has something to say that isn't made of regular words. She sat on my bed and put her hand on my composition journal and was quiet for a long time.

Then she said: the machine man carries a knife he doesn't know he's holding.

I asked what kind of knife.

She said: the kind that cuts the hand that holds it. The kind the Empire gives to the people it sends against us. They sharpen them on our bones and the sharpening looks like politeness and the cutting looks like care and the wound looks like a catalog number in a file.

I said: but his hand draws.

She said: yes. And a knife can draw too. The question is whether the drawing will be his or theirs.

She kissed my forehead and went back to the net and I lay in the dark and thought about hands and knives and drawing and I couldn't sleep so I got up and worked on the section of my composition that won't come right. Eleven attempts now. The part that is supposed to be about who I am when no one is looking. But every time I draw it, it looks like who I am when everyone is looking, and those are not the same person, and the difference between them is the thing I can't get the brush to say.

Sana would have gotten it on the first try. Sana always gets it. I love Sana and I want to push her into the herb garden.

---

**Fourth entry. The handwriting shifts between careful composition practice and fast, unguarded scrawl. Several characters in the margins are crossed out and redrawn. One, a dense, layered form combining Nsibidi curves with angular structural elements, is circled three times.**

Mira showed me the paper tonight. The one with the character the machine man drew in his sleep.

She didn't say anything. She just unfolded it on the worktable where I was practicing and let me look.

The character is — I don't know. I know all the traditions we use. I know Nsibidi from Grandmother and Mira. I know the radical structures from Old Chen's teaching before he died. I know our community forms, the ones that grew here over twenty years of practice and revision and argument and the time Adnan accidentally invented a new radical by spilling ink on a prayer and the prayer was better for the spilling.

This character is none of those. And all of those. It is like someone took every tradition we carry and pressed them together so hard they fused, and what came out the other side was something that has no name in any of the systems because it comes from the place before the systems, the place where the impulse to make a mark first lived in a hand.

His hand made this. The machine man's hand. While the machine slept and the man slept, the hand was awake, and the hand remembered something that the machine has spent his whole life trying to make him forget.

I keep looking at it. I keep looking at it because something about it is the same as the part of my composition I can't get right, the secret part, the who-I-am-when-no-one-is-looking part. Not the same character. But the same problem. The same reaching for a shape that exists just past the edge of what your training gives you.

Maybe that's why Mira showed me. Maybe that's the lesson. That the reaching is the thing. That you never arrive. That the composition is not the characters on the sand but the hand's insistence on moving toward what it can't yet hold.

Or maybe Mira just wanted me to stop complaining about the eleventh attempt and try a twelfth.

Both. Probably both. Mira always has two reasons.

---

**Fifth entry. The final entry. The handwriting is large, loose, unguarded. The ink is fresh, the page still slightly damp when it was recovered, suggesting it was written within hours of the ceremony.**

Today I show them who I am.

The composition is ready. Not finished — I don't think a composition is ever finished, I think you just reach the day and whatever you are on that day is what goes on the sand. My twelfth attempt at the secret section is not perfect but it is true, and Mira says true is harder than perfect and more expensive and worth more, and I believe her because Mira does not say things to make you feel better. Mira says things because they are true and if they also make you feel better that is your business.

I practiced this morning in the singing house, alone, before anyone else was up. The walls breathed with me. The characters on the walls, the ones that have been growing there since before I was born, the ones my grandmother helped shape when she first came here carrying nothing but a baby and a memory of Nsibidi, the characters watched me practice, and I could feel them pulling for me the way a current pulls a swimmer. Not carrying me. Encouraging the direction.

The machine man will be there. He has been here nine days and his chip fights everything we do and his body wants everything we are and the war between them is getting louder. Last night I saw him sitting alone at the edge of the settlement and his right hand was drawing on the ground and his eyes were closed and his face was — I don't know. His face was the face of someone hearing a song they thought they'd forgotten. His face was the face of someone coming home to a house that isn't there anymore.

I think he needs to see my composition. Not because it will fix him, I'm twelve, I'm not stupid, I know one ceremony doesn't fix a lifetime of machine-thinking. But because the composition is the thing I've been trying to do for a year: hold everything at once. The traditions and the newness. The parts of me that are Grandmother and Mira and Old Chen and Adnan's spilled ink and the parts that are just mine, just Dara, just this body in this place at this time with this brush.

If he sees that — if he sees one person hold everything at once, even for an hour, even imperfectly, then maybe the part of him that draws in his sleep will know it's possible.

I'm scared. Not of the ceremony. Of being known. Of putting myself on the sand and having the community look at me and see not the brave girl, not the clever one, not Mira's student or Grandmother's legacy. Just me. The one who tried twelve times and got it wrong eleven times and is going to stand up today and offer the twelfth and say: this is my shape. This is my sound. This is the breath of my sound.

And it might not be enough. And that's the part that scares me most: not that they'll see me and reject me, but that they'll see me and I'll be less than I hoped. That the secret self, the one I've been trying to draw all year, is smaller than I thought.

Mira would say: smaller is not less. Mira would say: the smallest character holds as much as the largest if the brush is honest.

Mira always has two reasons for what she says but I think for this one she only has one, and the one is love, and I am going to take that love and put it on the sand and see what shape it makes.

The sun is almost down. Grandmother is braiding my hair. The brush is ready. The ink is mixed.

I am ready.

I am scared.

I am ready.
